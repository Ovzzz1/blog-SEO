01

Entity Stacking

Linking branded profiles, references and citations across trusted sources and authority platforms to get Google to recognize brand identity — building consensus through neutral or positive mentions.

💡 Example — Stake

Stake fully succeeded in embedding themselves into the Knowledge Graph via multiple coordinated elements: Wikipedia page, About Page, Social networks, Press coverage. The key: perfect alignment across all mentioned information.

How to build a real consensus

The goal is to achieve a full, coherent alignment across all the places your brand is mentioned. Inconsistency between sources weakens the signal. Every mention should reinforce the same entity identity.

02

Authority vs Relevancy

The theoretical optimum is a DR 99 referring domain fully relevant to your niche — which is nearly impossible to achieve in practice. The real work is finding the best balance between the two.

⚠ Key Observation

Authority seems to outweigh relevancy — and this tendency is growing. Small entities and niche experts are being outranked by major authority players who are neither perfectly relevant nor targeting search intent well. Classic example: Forbes outranking healthcare providers on health-related SERPs.

Authority-first signal
A high-DR link from a semi-relevant domain often outperforms a low-DR link from a perfectly relevant one. Authority carries the floor.
Relevancy still matters
Relevancy amplifies authority when it's present. A DR 70 + relevant domain is close to optimal. Never ignore it entirely.
How to spot the trend
Reverse-engineer the SERPs by comparing Ahrefs data year-over-year. Evolution inspection is the most reliable signal.
Ranking shift
This authority-over-relevancy pattern applies both to linking and to general rankings. Factor it into your site-wide strategy.
03

Money Pages vs Supporting Pages vs Homepage

Money Pages

Before building any links to money pages, analyse the SERP to understand what consensus you need to fit. Just like content needs to match the intent and topical consensus, linking needs to mirror what the SERP's top players actually look like.

💡 Floate's Principle

As much as we want to fit the consensus on the SERP through content, we need to do so through linking too.

Having links when they're not needed delivers negative ROI at best, risk at worst. Money pages should always receive the safest and most powerful links in your arsenal.

Supporting Pages

Supporting pages (typically blog content) are useful because they can distribute link equity via multiple internal links — and you can afford to be more aggressive when acquiring links towards them.

⚠ Cluster Rule

Use a supporting page from a given cluster to push money pages of that same cluster only. Link equity shouldn't bleed out of its cluster. A sports betting supporting page should push the sports betting LP — not casino, not horse bet.

If bad links hit a supporting page, the damage stays there rather than directly hitting the money page. However, if volume is high enough, the negative effect will become site-wide regardless.

Homepage

The homepage is the foundation of trust and site-wide authority. When homepage authority increases, the entire website theoretically benefits. It's the primary target for entity stacking, fake interviews, quoting and brand mentions.

Safe
Safest + most powerful links only. SERP consensus first.
Supporting Pages
Flexible
More aggressive allowed. Same cluster rule applies.

How to Pick Anchor Text

Anchor ratios and percentages are overrated and largely useless. The more important factor is naturalness — especially the paragraph surrounding the link.

✓ Core Rule

The paragraph around the link needs to be clean, semantically rich and contextually relevant to the linked page. The best way to achieve this is through proper human editing — not templated insertion.

Capitalization
One of the most common spam signals Google uses. "Stake is the Best Online Casino" — capitalized anchors get flagged. Stay natural.
Competitive Match
Most competitive anchor → most competitive page. Match anchor aggressiveness to page target intentionally.
Anchor Diversity
Mix optimized, semi-optimized, branded, URL and generic anchors. No single type should dominate.
Context > Anchor
A well-written surrounding paragraph with a generic anchor often outperforms an exact-match anchor dropped into thin content.

How to Avoid Anti-Spam Policy

Rank your guest posts

Having guest posts that actually rank and receive direct signals from Google is far more valuable than anything else. Getting links on existing, already-ranking pages is a massive lever. Tier 2 linking on blog posts is one effective way to both make posts rank and offset potential spam signals.

❌ Avoid

Linking to orphan pages. Paid pages in particular absolutely need other forms of link equity through internal linking. An orphan paid page is a red flag.

Full Diversification Framework

Diversification goes way beyond the dofollow/nofollow split. It needs to be systematic across every dimension:

Link Velocity

Velocity is hard to manage because it depends on site-specific factors: age, authority, page count, current linking baseline. A few key principles:

  • Do not count pillow links in your velocity strategy — they operate at a different layer.
  • Don't over-accelerate — avoid having dramatically more links than new articles being published.
  • Be logical and natural — velocity spikes that don't match the site's content output are easy to identify algorithmically.
06

How to Vet a Website Before Linking From It

19-point checklist — from editorial quality to ROI. Every referring domain should pass through this before acquisition.

01Publishing relevance — Are they currently publishing content relevant to your niche? Not unrelated or flagged topics (casino, adult…)?
02Publishing rhythm — 1 article/day vs 10/day changes the value of each post significantly.
03Niche relevance — Is the site actually relevant to your vertical?
04Content depth — Thin, low-quality content? Or rich, entity-filled, genuinely useful articles?
05Ranking velocity — Do they rank quickly? Do they rank at all?
06Wayback Machine check — Has the site kept the same thematic focus over time? Did someone buy an expired domain and pivot it?
07Redirect history — Has the domain ever had significant redirects?
08Spam signals — Chinese, Indonesian or other spammy patterns in the backlink profile?
09Traffic & link sources — Check traffic directories and source quality.
10Fake traffic — Is the traffic real? Is it relevant to your niche?
11DR quality — Is DR inflated by redirects pointing to the homepage? Check UR of internal pages vs homepage DR gap.
12Content quality — Fully AI-generated? Still acceptable if it ranks, but slightly less optimal.
13Internal linking — Is content internally linked properly or are pages isolated?
14Enrichment signals — Schema, microdata or other structured data present?
15Formatting — Font issues, broken Hn hierarchy, wall-of-text, blank spaces — all negative signals.
16Page/traffic ratio — 2,000 pages / 2,000 traffic = bad. 100 pages / 2,000 traffic = excellent. Higher perceived value per page.
17ROI potential — What's the expected return vs price for this geo / language / niche combination?
18Risk assessment — Aggregate of all previous signals. What's the overall risk level of linking from here?
19Publisher policy — How many links allowed? Images? Internal links? What can you actually get for your article?
🔬 Key ratio to remember

The page/traffic ratio (point 16) is one of the strongest signals of domain quality. A site with 100 pages and 2K traffic has high perceived page value — your sponsored article has a much better chance of ranking on it than on a 2K-page site with the same traffic.
